I get my septoplasty splints out in less than 3 hours, thank god. 6 days ago I had a septoplasty and turbinate reduction and for the most part, the after effects were mild. I suffered very little pain, no post-anesthesia nausea, the expected amount of bleeding. The worst part was just having to live with stents blocking each nostril. Every night was restless and uncomfortable. The brain fog that arose from mouth breathing all day and night was thick. It _feels_ like there are two giant, plastic stents sandwiching my septum because there is.

---

Its nice, in a morbid humor kind of way, to be reminded of what you lost. Before having my surgery, I was hardly burdened with the thought of nose breathing. That's not to say nose breathing was easy. I could do it (as I'm realizing now), it just wasn't perfect. I could sleep through an entire night with my mouth literally taped shut and still nail a 95 sleep score so you could say that my nose worked okay. But now, after nearly a week of terrible nose clearance, that okay becomes fantastic. Its the simple idea that you don't appreciate what you have until its gone. Its true. As soon as something in your life gets worse, whether that be your health, your financial situation, your ego, your relationships, you notice it and would always prefer that they didn't get worse. I'm speaking the obvious.

But, what makes this appreciation for the once-was so interesting is that its usually discussed as something permanent. You lost an eye and now you wish for your 3D vision to return. You lost a loved one and wish they could be revived out of the grave. You were fired from your job for substance abuse and wish you never hit that happy K. What if you could fake this pastward appreciation to amplify your gratitude for what you have now? This seems like the perfect use for a ChatGPT wrapper or custom GPT, something that can temporarily guide you through a bad fantasy and back to the present. Say for example I wanted to appreciate something I never think about, and thus wouldn't be able to prompt gippity to coach me on. The app could select from any number of terrible misfortunes documented on the internet and mold the story to fit my life. Aside from the fact that this could be seen as manifesting the negative, it could work wonders. I want to appreciate my normal, unremarkable feet so gippity takes me to a dream world where I have hobbit feet and can mentally experience the discomfort of packing those potatoes into Nikes...the more I describe this, the more unpleasant the entire idea seems.

Regardless, I will have my normal life back in 3 hours and I'm excited to rekindle my love for life and health and routine.
